Problem 01

Consider a situation where a manufacturing company wants to get a better idea about the mean performance of a part they produce. Unfortunately, measuring the performance of that part requires a destructive experiment. Therefore, they would like to test as few parts as possible. Engineers within the company have performed experiments in the past, and so they hope that experience can help in some way.

To help out this company, we will assume that the part performance can be represented by a Gaussian likelihood, given the mean performance \(\mu\) and the noise \(\sigma\). We will assume the observation of each experiment, \(x_{n}\), is conditionally independent given those hyperparameters. The joint distribution between \(N\) observations can therefore be factored into the product of \(N\) separate likelihoods:

\[ p\left( \mathbf{x} \mid \mu, \sigma \right) = \prod_{n=1}^{N} \{ \mathrm{normal}\left(x_{n} \mid \mu, \sigma \right) \} \]

1a)

In order to perform a Bayesian analysis, we will need to specify our prior belief on the unknown parameters. The parameter the engineers are most concerned about is \(\mu\). Let’s represent our prior belief on \(\mu\) with a Gaussian prior parameterized by \(\mu_{0}\) and \(\tau_{0}\). After asking multiple engineers, it seems that the prior \(\pm 2\) standard deviation interval or roughly 95% uncertainty interval around the prior mean, \(\mu_{0}\) is between 115 units and 135 units.

Based on the interval suggested by the engineers, solve for the \(\mu_{0}\) and \(\tau_{0}\) hyperparameters for the Gaussian prior on the mean, \(\mu\).

SOLUTION

The statement that \(\pm2\) prior standard deviations is between 115 and 135 can be written as:

\[ 115 = \mu_{0} - 2 \tau_{0} \\ 135 = \mu_{0} + 2 \tau_{0} \]

Rearrange the first expression to write \(\mu_{0}\) in terms of $_{0}:

\[ \mu_{0} = 115 + 2 \tau_{0} \]

Substitute the above equality into the second expression within the first equation block:

\[ 135 = \left( 115 + 2 \tau_{0} \right) + 2\tau_0 = 115 + 4 \tau_{0} \]

Which allows solving for \(\tau_{0}\):

\[ 135 - 115 = 4 \tau_0 \\ 20 = 4 \tau_0 \\ \tau_0 = \frac{20}{4} \\ \tau_0 = 5 \]

Substituting \(\tau_0\) back into the expresion for \(\mu_0\) gives:

\[ \mu_0 = 115 + 2 \times \tau_0 = 115 + 2 \times 5 = 115 + 10 \\ \mu_0 = 125 \]

Thus, the hyperprior on \(\mu\) is a Gaussian with prior mean \(\mu_0 = 125\) and prior standard deviation \(\tau_0 = 5\). Note that, if you wanted to be exact and use the middle 95% uncertainty interval is \(\pm 1.96\) standard deviations around the mean your final numbers would be slightly different.

Based on your result in Problem 1a), calculate the 25th and 75th quantiles associated with the prior Gaussian on \(\mu\). The interval between these two quantiles contains what fraction of the probabilty mass?

SOLUTION

1d)

Most of the engineers feel confident that the process standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is known. They believe that \(\sigma = 5\) units. We will now use this value of \(\sigma\) to estimate the required number of observations to achieve a target posterior standard deviation on the mean performance, \(\mu\).

Based on \(\sigma = 5\) and your result in Problem 1a), how many observations are required to have a posterior standard deviation on \(\mu\) to be \(\tau_{N} = 2\)? How many observations are required to have \(\tau_{N} = 0.5\)?

You should write out the expression for the number of required observations to achieve this goal and then calculate the values associated with two different \(\tau_{N}\) values.

SOLUTION

We need to use the expression for the posterior precision discussed in lecture. The posterior precision for the normal likelihood with a conjugate normal prior and known standard deviation is the sum of the prior precision and the data precision:

\[ \frac{1}{\tau_{N}^{2}} = \frac{1}{\tau_{0}^2} + \frac{N}{\sigma^2} \]

We can rearrange the above expression to solve for \(N\) in terms of the known \(\sigma\), the assumed \(\tau_0\) and the desired \(\tau_N\):

\[ N = \sigma^2 \times \left(\frac{1}{\tau_{N}^{2}} - \frac{1}{\tau_{0}^{2}} \right) \]

We can now easily calculate the required number of observations to achieve the target posterior precision of 2:

5^2 * ((1/2^2) - (1/5^2))
## [1] 5.25

Thus, we would need 6 fully tested parts (since we can’t test a fraction of a part) to get the posterior standard deviation less than 2 units.

If we instead wanted the target posterior standard deviation to be 0.5 units:

5^2 * ((1/0.5^2) - (1/5^2))
## [1] 99

We would need to test 99 parts! The simple formula for the posterior precision allowed us to get a sense about the relationship of sample size and target levels of precision.

Problem 02

In lecture, we introduced the normal-normal model with an unknown mean, \(\mu\), and known standard deviation, \(\sigma\). Although we discussed the interpretation of the posterior distribution when using the conjugate normal prior on \(\mu\), we did not fully derive the posterior. In this problem, you will get practice working with Gaussians by deriving the expression for the posterior mode or Max A-Posteriori (MAP) estimate. You do not need to complete the square to solve this problem. Instead, you will find the MAP by optimizing the un-normalized log-posterior with respect to \(\mu\). The recording and notes provided on Canvas which show how to derive the Maximum Likelihood Estimate (MLE) may be helpful with this problem.

This problem uses the same nomenclature as lecture. The prior on \(\mu\) is a Gaussian with prior mean \(\mu_{0}\) and prior standard deviation \(\tau_{0}\).

2a)

The posterior on \(\mu\) given \(N\) observations \(\mathbf{x}\) and known standard deviation \(\sigma\) is proportional to:

\[ p\left(\mu \mid \mathbf{x}, \sigma\right) \propto \prod_{n=1}^{N} \left( \mathrm{normal} \left( x_n \mid \mu, \sigma \right) \right) \times \mathrm{normal}\left( \mu \mid \mu_0, \tau_0\right) \]

The denominator in Bayes’ rule is ignored in the above expression. Because of that I will typically refer to the expression above as the un-normalized posterior.

Write out the expression for the un-normalized log-posterior on \(\mu\). You may drop all terms that do not involve \(\mu\). You should show several steps and not simply write down the final answer.

SOLUTION

Let’s start out by considering the log-likelihood associated with the \(n\)-th observation:

\[ \log \left( \mathrm{normal}\left(x_n \mid \mu, \sigma \right) \right) = -\frac{1}{2} \log \left(2\pi\right) - \frac{1}{2} \log\left(\sigma^2\right) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \left(x_n - \mu\right)^2 \]

And then let’s drop all terms that do not involve \(\mu\):

\[ \log \left( \mathrm{normal}\left(x_n \mid \mu, \sigma \right) \right) \propto - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \left(x_n - \mu\right)^2 \]

We then sum over the \(N\) observations to get the log-likelihood up to a normalizing constant:

\[ \log \left(p\left( \mathbf{x} \mid \mu, \sigma \right) \right) \propto -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left( \left(x_n - \mu\right)^2 \right) \]

We start out similarly with the log-prior:

\[ \log\left( \mathrm{normal} \left( \mu \mid \mu_0, \tau_0 \right) \right) = -\frac{1}{2} \log \left(2\pi\right) - \frac{1}{2} \log\left(\tau_0^2\right) - \frac{1}{2\tau_0^2} \left(\mu - \mu_0\right)^2 \]

Drop all terms that do not involve \(\mu\) to yield the log-prior up to a normalizing constant:

\[ \log\left( \mathrm{normal} \left( \mu \mid \mu_0, \tau_0 \right) \right) \propto- \frac{1}{2\tau_0^2} \left(\mu - \mu_0\right)^2 \]

Sum the log-likelihood with the log-prior to yield the log-posterior up to a normalizing constant:

\[ \log \left( p\left( \mu \mid \mathbf{x}, \sigma \right) \right) \propto -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left( \left( x_n - \mu \right)^2 \right) - \frac{1}{2\tau_{0}^2} \left( \mu - \mu_0 \right)^2 \]

2b)

Derive the derivative of the un-normalized log-posterior with respect to \(\mu\). Your final answer should be written in terms of the sufficient statistic, the sample average \(\bar{x}\).

SOLUTION

Differentiation is a linear operator, thus we can apply the derivative within the summation series:

\[ \frac{d}{d\mu} \left( \log \left( p\left( \mu \mid \mathbf{x}, \sigma \right) \right) \right) = -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left( \frac{d}{d\mu}\left( \left( x_n - \mu \right)^2 \right) \right) - \frac{1}{2\tau_{0}^2} \frac{d}{d\mu}\left( \left( \mu - \mu_0 \right)^2 \right) \]

Written this way, we see that we only need to calculate two derivatives, the first is the \(n\)-th observation’s contribution within the summation series, and the second is associated with the log-prior. Both have similar functional forms in that they are quadratic (due to the Gaussian likelihood and Gaussian prior), but the “position” of the unknown is different between the two. Thus, the signs of the result should be different.

Let’s start with the term associated with the likelihood:

\[ \frac{d}{d\mu} \left( \left(x_n - \mu\right)^2 \right) = -2 \times \left(x_n - \mu \right) \]

And likewise for the log-prior term:

\[ \frac{d}{d\mu} \left( \left(\mu - \mu_0\right)^2 \right) = 2 \times \left(\mu - \mu_0 \right) \]

Placing those terms back into the original expression involving the summation and the other coefficients, the derivative can be written as:

\[ \frac{d}{d\mu} \left( \log \left( p\left( \mu \mid \mathbf{x}, \sigma \right) \right) \right) = -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left( -2 \times \left(x_n - \mu \right) \right) - \frac{1}{2\tau_{0}^2} \times 2 \times \left(\mu - \mu_0 \right) \]

Simplify:

\[ \frac{d}{d\mu} \left( \log \left( p\left( \mu \mid \mathbf{x}, \sigma \right) \right) \right) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left(\left(x_n - \mu \right) \right) - \frac{1}{\tau_{0}^2} \left(\mu - \mu_0 \right) \]

The summation series can be simplified by “distributing” the summation over the individual terms:

\[ \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left(\left(x_n - \mu \right) \right) = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left(x_n\right) - \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left(\mu\right) \]

The second term on the right hand side is simply a summation of a constant, \(\mu\), and thus simplifies to:

\[ \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left(\mu\right) = N \times \mu \]

The summation of the \(N\) observations can be simplified by using the sample average, which is defined as:

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left(x_n\right) \]

Thus, the summation series is the sample average multiplied by the number of observations:

\[ \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left(x_n\right) = N \times \bar{x} \]

These quantities can be placed back in the expression for the derivative:

\[ \frac{d}{d\mu} \left( \log \left( p\left( \mu \mid \mathbf{x}, \sigma \right) \right) \right) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \left( N\bar{x} - N \mu \right) - \frac{1}{\tau_{0}^2} \left(\mu - \mu_0 \right) \]

Which simplifies to the final result:

\[ \frac{d}{d\mu} \left( \log \left( p\left( \mu \mid \mathbf{x}, \sigma \right) \right) \right) = \frac{N}{\sigma^2} \left( \bar{x} - \mu \right) - \frac{1}{\tau_{0}^2} \left(\mu - \mu_0 \right) \]

2c)

Determine the posterior mode or MAP by setting the derivative expression you determined in Problem 2b) to 0. Denote the posterior mode on \(\mu\) as \(\mu_{MAP}\).

SOLUTION

The posterior mode is found by setting the first derivative to 0 and solving for \(\mu\):

\[ \frac{d}{d\mu} \left. \left( \log \left( p\left( \mu \mid \mathbf{x}, \sigma \right) \right) \right) \right|_{\mu_{MAP}} = 0 = \frac{N}{\sigma^2} \left( \bar{x} - \mu_{MAP} \right) - \frac{1}{\tau_{0}^2} \left(\mu_{MAP} - \mu_0 \right) \]

Rearrange the right hand side by grouping the terms that involve \(\mu_{MAP}\):

\[ \frac{N}{\sigma^2} \left( \bar{x} - \mu_{MAP} \right) - \frac{1}{\tau_{0}^2} \left(\mu_{MAP} - \mu_0 \right) = \frac{N}{\sigma^2} \bar{x} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2}\mu_0 - \left( \frac{N}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2} \right) \mu_{MAP} = 0 \]

Rearrange by moving the term associated with the parantheses to the other side.

\[ \frac{N}{\sigma^2} \bar{x} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2}\mu_0 = \left( \frac{N}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2} \right) \mu_{MAP} \]

The posterior mode is therefore:

\[ \mu_{MAP} = \frac{ \frac{N}{\sigma^2} \bar{x} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2} \mu_0} {\frac{N}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2}} \]

2d)

Your expression for the posterior mode should look familiar. In fact, we worked with the expression for the MAP in lecture, but we referred to that expression by a different name.

Your answer in Problem 2c) should be equivalent to an expression provided in lecture. Which expression is it the same as? Why is your result for the posterior mode the same as that expression? Think in terms of the type of distribution we know the posterior to be given the problem formulation.

SOLUTION

The posterior mode or MAP expression we derived in Problem 2c) is identical to the expression we discussed for the posterior mean, \(\mu_{N}\), in lecture. The two expressions are the same because we used a conjugate Normal prior on the unknown mean, \(\mu\), with our Normal likelihood. The normal is conjugate to the normal, and thus the posterior distribution is also a Gaussian! The mode of a Gaussian is equal to its mean (and also its median). That is why \(\mu_{MAP} = \mu_{N}\)!

Problem 03

In lecture, we discussed learning an unknown mean of a normal likelihood, given a series of observations. We compared the MLE to the Bayesian result with a conjugate normal prior, assuming the measurement standard deviation (the likelihood noise) was known. We qualitatively and graphically discussed the more complicated situation of an unknown mean and unknown noise. We will continue that discussion in our next lecture, where we will see how to approximate the joint posterior with the Laplace Approximation.

As a stepping stone to the two unknown parameter model, you will work with a normal likelihood but switching which parameters are known and unknown. Thus, you will work with a normal likelihood with a known mean, \(\mu\), while the standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is unknown. The \(N\) observations (or measurements) are still denoted as a vector, \(\mathbf{x}\).

The posterior distribution on the unknown \(\sigma\) given the observations, \(\mathbf{x}\), and the known mean, \(\mu\), is proportional to:

\[ p \left(\sigma \mid \mathbf{x}, \mu\right) \propto p\left(\mathbf{x} \mid \mu, \sigma \right) p\left(\sigma\right) \]

We will continue to assume all observations are conditionally independent given the parameters. The likelihood therefore factors into the product of \(N\) normal likelihoods. Thus, the posterior is proportional to:

\[ p \left(\sigma \mid \mathbf{x}, \mu\right) \propto \prod_{n=1}^{N} \left(\mathrm{normal}\left( x_n \mid \mu, \sigma \right) \right) \times p\left(\sigma\right) \]

Notice that the prior distribution must be placed on \(\sigma\). The example discussed in lecture used a Uniform distribution as the prior on \(\sigma\). However, in this assignment you will work with a different distribution, one that we will work with more when we start fitting regression models. You will use an Exponential distribution as the prior on \(\sigma\). The shorthand notation for the Exponential distribution is usually \(\mathrm{Exp}\), and I will always use a capital E to denote the distribution and not the exponential function \(\exp()\). The Exponential distribution has a single hyperparameter, known as the rate parameter and is sometimes denoted as \(\lambda\). The Exponential probability density function with rate \(\lambda\) is:

\[ \mathrm{Exp}\left(\sigma \mid \lambda\right) = \lambda \exp \left(-\lambda \sigma \right) \]

The un-normalized posterior on the unknown likelihood noise, \(\sigma\), is therefore:

\[ p \left(\sigma \mid \mathbf{x}, \mu\right) \propto \prod_{n=1}^{N} \left(\mathrm{normal}\left( x_n \mid \mu, \sigma \right) \right) \times \mathrm{Exp}\left( \sigma \mid \lambda \right) \]

3a)

You will now start working with the un-normalized log-posterior for unknown \(\sigma\).

You must derive the expression for the un-normalized log-posterior on \(\sigma\). You may drop the constant terms and terms that do not directly involve \(\sigma\). You do not need to simplify the quadratic portion within the likelihood.

SOLUTION

Start by plugging in the expression for the normal likelihood for each observation and the Exponential density.

\[ p \left(\sigma \mid \mathbf{x}, \mu \right) \propto \prod_{n=1}^{N} \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \left(x_n - \mu\right)^2 \right) \right) \times \lambda \exp \left(-\lambda \sigma \right) \]

Apply the natural log:

\[ \log \left( p \left(\sigma \mid \mathbf{x}, \mu \right) \right) \propto \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left( -\frac{1}{2} \log\left(2\pi \right) -\frac{1}{2} \log\left(\sigma^2 \right) -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \left(x_n - \mu \right)^2 \right) + \log\left(\lambda\right) -\lambda \sigma \]

Expand the summation through the times in the likelihood:

\[ \log \left( p \left(\sigma \mid \mathbf{x}, \mu \right) \right) \propto \sum_{n=1}^{N}\left(-\frac{1}{2} \log\left(2\pi\right)\right) + \sum_{n=1}^{N} \left( -\frac{1}{2} \log \left(\sigma^2\right)\right) -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{n=1}^{N} \left( \left(x_n - \mu \right)^2 \right) + \log\left(\lambda\right) -\lambda\sigma \]

Drop the terms that are not associated with the unknown \(\sigma\):

\[ \log \left( p \left(\sigma \mid \mathbf{x}, \mu \right) \right) \propto -\frac{N}{2} \log \left( \sigma^2 \right) -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{n=1}^{N} \left( \left(x_n - \mu \right)^2 \right) -\lambda\sigma \]

3b)

When we discussed the normal-normal model for the unknown mean, we talked about the sufficient statistic being the sample average. In this situation, an unknown standard deviation with a known mean, we also have a sufficient statistic, but it is not the sample average. The sufficient statistic is defined for you in the equation block below:

\[ v = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N}\left( \left(x_n - \mu\right)^2 \right) \]

Simplify your result from Problem 3a) by making use of the sufficient statistic, \(v\). Why do you think the quantity \(v\) is referred to as being “sufficient”? Does your expression for the un-normalized log-posterior depend on the individual observations after making use of \(v\)?

SOLUTION

The expression for the log-posterior greatly simplifies when we make use of the sufficient statistic. To see how, let’s rewrite the sufficient statistic expression as:

\[ Nv = \sum_{n=1}^{N}\left( \left(x_n - \mu\right)^2 \right) \]

We can now see that the summation series term is equal to the product of the number of observations, \(N\), and the sufficient statistic, \(v\). Let’s substitute the above expression into the expression for log-posterior on \(\sigma\):

\[ \log \left( p\left( \sigma \mid \mathbf{x}, \mu \right) \right) \propto -\frac{N}{2} \log \left( \sigma^2\right) - \frac{Nv}{2\sigma^2} - \lambda \sigma \]

“Sufficient” represents that all information content from the data is contained within that single value. The log-posterior does not require the individual observations. We simply need to know the quantity \(v\).

3e)

Next week in lecture, we will work with the quadratic or Laplace Approximation to approximate the posterior distribution as a Multivariate Normal. Although this can be a useful approximation, it is not appropriate for this type of situation. The likelihood noise, \(\sigma\), has a natural lower bound of 0. A Gaussian distribution has no such constraints, and so allows all values from negative infinity to positive infinity! Therefore, even though the prior Exponential distribution respects the bound on \(\sigma\), if we would use the Laplace Approximation to approximate the posterior, the lower bound would be violated!

We need to make use of a transformation in order to overcome this limitation. Since the likelihood noise is lower bounded at 0, we will apply a log-transformation. The transformed variable, \(\varphi\), will be equal to the log of \(\sigma\). Thus, the transformation or link function, \(g\left(\right)\), is equal to the log function:

\[ \varphi = g\left(\sigma\right) = \log \left(\sigma\right) \]

The change-of-variables formula, written in terms of the log-density, is given below:

\[ \log\left(p_{\varphi} \left(\varphi \mid \mathbf{x}, \mu\right) \right) = \log \left( p_{\sigma} \left( g^{-1} \left(\varphi \right) \mid \mathbf{x}, \mu \right) \right) + \log \left( \left| \frac{d}{d\varphi} \left( g^{-1}\left(\varphi\right) \right) \right| \right) \]

This may seem like an intimidating expression, but it simply says plug in the expression for \(\sigma\) in terms of \(\varphi\) (the inverse of the link function) into the log-posterior expression on \(\sigma\). Then add in the log of the derivative of the inverse link function with respect to \(\varphi\). The recording and notes provided on Canvas about performing change-of-variables with the simple linear z-score transformation can help with further discussion and practice working with the change-of-variables procedure.

Let’s tackle this problem by first determining the log of the derivative of the inverse link function.

The link function is the log. What is the inverse link function? Write down the expression for calculating \(\sigma\) as a function of \(\varphi\). Then calculate the derivative of the inverse link function with respect to \(\varphi\). Finally, write down the log of the derivative of the inverse link function.

SOLUTION

The inverse link function is the inverse of the log, which is the exponential. Thus, the inverse link function is:

\[ \sigma = g^{-1}\left( \varphi \right) = \exp \left( \varphi \right) \]

The derivative of the inverse link function is simply the derivative of the exponential of \(\varphi\) with respect \(\varphi\). The derivative is therefore simply equal to function itself!

\[ \frac{d}{d\varphi} \left( \exp \left( \varphi \right) \right) = \exp \left( \varphi \right) \]

The exponential of a quantity is always a positive number, so the absolute value equals the exponential.

The log is the inverse operation of the exponential function, and so the log of the derivative is simply the \(\varphi\)!

\[ \log \left( \exp \left(\varphi \right) \right) = \varphi \]

Thus, the complicated looking log-derivative is simply just \(\varphi\)!

\[ \log \left( \left| \frac{d}{d\varphi} \left( g^{-1}\left(\varphi\right) \right) \right| \right) = \varphi \]
